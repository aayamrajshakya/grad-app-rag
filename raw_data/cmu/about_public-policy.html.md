CyLab researchers are often called upon by government leaders to provide expertise on security and privacy issues. Below are a few highlight examples of our researchers engaging with policymakers in federal and local governments.
## Leadership roles in Washington, D.C.
  * In 2016-2017, CyLab director [Lorrie Cranor](https://cylab.cmu.edu/directory/bios/cranor-lorrie.html) served as Chief Technologist at the Federal Trade Commission. 
  * In 2008-2010, [Jon Peha](https://cylab.cmu.edu/directory/bios/peha-jon.html) served as the Chief Technologist of the Federal Communications Commission. Peha also served in the White House as the Assistant Director in the Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP) in 2010-2011, in the House Energy & Commerce Committee, and at USAID where he helped launch and lead a US Government interagency program to assist developing countries with information infrastructure.


## Congressional testimonies
  * In 2018, [Nicolas Christin](https://cylab.cmu.edu/directory/bios/christin-nicolas.html) gave a congressional testimony on cybercrime in a hearing entitled, “After the Breach: The Monetization and Illicit Use of Stolen Data.”
  * In 2010, [Lorrie Cranor](https://cylab.cmu.edu/directory/bios/cranor-lorrie.html) gave a testimony before the United States House of Representatives, Energy and Commerce Committee Subcommittee on Communications, Technology and the Internet, and Subcommittee on Commerce, Trade, and Consumer Protection, Hearing on The Collection and Use of Location Information for Commercial Purposes.

## CyLab research informing policymakers
  * In 2022, Lorrie Cranor was invited to speak at the Federal Communications Commission virtual public hearing on broadband consumer labels. CyLab later responded to the FCC's request for comment by conducting a large-scale user study of more than 2,500 participants, uncovering the information most important to consumers shopping for broadband internet service and determining what terminology and presentation formats make this information most understandable and useful.
  * In 2022, [Yuvraj Agarwal](https://www.cylab.cmu.edu/directory/bios/agarwal-yuvraj.html) presented [CyLab's IoT security and privacy research](https://www.iotsecurityprivacy.org) at the White House’s Internet of Things Security Summit.
  * In 2020, researchers in the CyLab Usable Privacy and Security (CUPS) Laboratory[designed a privacy icon](https://cylab.cmu.edu/news/2020/12/11-donotsell.html)
  * In 2019, Lorrie Cranor’s research on usable and useful privacy interfaces[was a leading paper](https://fpf.org/2019/12/05/privacy-papers-2019/) in the DC-based Future of Privacy Forum's (FPF) Privacy Papers for Policy Makers (PPPM)
  * In 2015, Lorrie Cranor’s research on effective privacy notices[was a leading paper](https://fpf.org/privacy-papers-for-policy-makers/privacy-papers-2015/)
  * In 2012, Lorrie Cranor’s research on online behavior tracking[was a leading paper](https://fpf.org/privacy-papers-for-policy-makers/privacy-papers-2012/)
  * In 2010, CyLab research papers[made up half of the leading papers](https://fpf.org/privacy-papers-for-policy-makers/privacy-papers-2010/)


## Collaborations with state governments
  * Following the implementation of the California Consumer Protection Act (CCPA) in early 2020, the CyLab Usable Privacy and Security (CUPS) Lab conducted a [series of studies towards making privacy choices easier for consumers to find and exercise](http://cups.cs.cmu.edu/optout.html). One [study](http://cups.cs.cmu.edu/pubs/CCPA2020Feb24.pdf) evaluated the California Attorney General (AG) Office’s proposed opt-out button and submitted the report to the California AG.
  * In 2016, the California AG’s Office [piloted Norman Sadeh’s research group’s Mobile App Privacy Compliance tool](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-kamala-d-harris-launches-new-tool-help-consumers-report).
